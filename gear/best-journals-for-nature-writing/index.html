The bottom line

The journal you take outside needs to survive getting wet, survive being dropped, and hold up to pencil, ballpoint, and ideally watercolor. Rite in the Rain is the only option if weather is unpredictable. Leuchtturm1917 is the best all-purpose journal for fair-weather field use. Field Notes is the best pocket companion for quick field notes. Moleskine's watercolor sketchbook is the pick if you want to paint what you observe. And for paper quality alone, the Stillman & Birn Beta spiral-bound is what serious nature journalers use. None of these are wrong choices — the right one is the one you actually take outside.

A nature journal is not the same object as a bedside journal. It has to go in a pack, a pocket, a glovebox. It needs to survive a rainstorm on the trail, coffee rings from an outdoor cafe, being sat on, being written in with frozen hands. The beautiful cream-paper Rhodia that works beautifully at your desk has no business on a hiking trail in November.

But beyond the functional requirements, the nature journal serves a specific purpose: slowing down enough to actually observe. The act of writing or drawing what you notice forces a different quality of attention than photograph-and-move. Naturalists and field scientists have known this for centuries. The journal is not a record of what you saw — it's the instrument of seeing more deeply.

📊 The Research

Ecologist Robin Wall Kimmerer describes nature journaling's core mechanism in Braiding Sweetgrass: "In writing about nature, we must attend to it — must sit with it long enough to have something to say." Research on field journaling confirms this: people who keep written observations of natural environments show significantly greater retention of ecological detail and generate more specific, accurate observations than those who only photograph. Writing makes you look harder. Looking harder makes you see more.

The act of writing forces you to look harder. Looking harder means you see more. That's the whole practice.

What a Field Journal Needs

The ideal nature journal has: a cover that can take abuse, paper that handles pencil and ink (and ideally some water without cockle-warping), a size that fits in a pack pocket, and ideally lies flat when open for writing outdoors. Most fine notebooks fail at least one of these.

The additional consideration: you want something you don't feel precious about. The expensive Rhodia that you hate to get a mud smear on is not the nature journal. The journal that goes outside without anxiety — that's the one.

Rite in the Rain — The Indestructible Option

Rite in the Rain makes weatherproof paper. Not water-resistant — weatherproof. You can write on it in rain. Submerge it and the paper doesn't disintegrate. The military uses it. Field biologists use it. Trail crews use it. If there's any chance of precipitation, this is the journal.

The spiral-bound 4¾" × 7½" is the classic field format. Fits in a hip pocket, lies flat, writes well with ballpoint or pencil (not fountain pen — the coating resists wet ink). The pocket-sized 3½" × 5" is the one to keep in a jacket pocket always.

Leuchtturm1917 — Best for Mixed Use

If you do most of your nature writing in fair weather and want a journal that also works at a desk, the Leuchtturm1917 A5 dotted is the move. Good paper (80gsm, handles most inks), numbered pages, proper index in the front. The dotted grid works well for a combination of writing and field sketches.

This is the journal for the person who records observations in the field and also processes them — writes longer reflections, sketches details, makes sense of what they saw — back at camp or home. The structured approach of the Leuchtturm rewards that kind of integrated use.

Moleskine Watercolor Sketchbook

Nature journaling and watercolor painting are natural companions — John Muir's journals famously combined written observation with botanical illustrations, and the tradition continues in the modern field naturalist community. Moleskine's watercolor sketchbook has 200gsm cold-press paper specifically designed to handle wet media. The pages don't cockle (buckle with water) the way standard notebook paper does.

This is the journal for people who want to paint what they observe — birds, plants, landscapes. The paper handles pencil, ink, and watercolor without complaint. Heavier and more expensive than a standard journal, but for the right purpose, entirely worth it.

Field Notes — The Pocket Companion

Field Notes earned their name for a reason: they're small enough to always have and durable enough to take anywhere. The 3.5" × 5.5" format lives in a hip pocket or jacket inner pocket. Use them for quick field observations — a species you want to look up, a behavior you want to remember, a detail of light at a specific time. Transfer the good stuff to a larger journal later.

The paper doesn't handle wet ink well, but pencil and ballpoint are excellent. The lined editions are best for text-heavy observation; the graph edition works if you want to sketch proportions and shapes.

How to Start Nature Journaling (Even If You Can't Draw)

The most common reason people don't start a nature journal: "I can't draw." This is the wrong frame. Nature journaling is not an art practice, though it can become one. It's an observation practice. You can nature journal entirely in words.

Start with time and place. Every entry: date, time, location, weather. This creates the context that makes the observation meaningful later.

Describe what you notice, not what you know. "Small brown bird with rusty-orange tail, fanned out repeatedly while perching on a fence post" is better observation than "Eastern phoebe." Write what your senses tell you before reaching for the identification.

Make lists. "What I see, hear, smell" is a legitimate and valuable entry. The lists accumulate into a picture of a place over time.

Sketch badly. Even crude sketches — proportions, basic shapes, placement of features — train your eye in ways that writing alone doesn't. You don't have to be good. You have to try.

Sean, high school biology teacher — Washington

"I started keeping a nature journal on hikes, mostly writing descriptions of what I noticed. Two years later I have a record of nearly 400 observations in my local park. I've started recognizing seasonal patterns, behaviors, plant phenology. The journal didn't just record what I noticed — it made me notice more."
