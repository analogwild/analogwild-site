The bottom line

For most beginners: start with a Kodak or Fujifilm disposable ($15) to see if you love the feel, then graduate to an Olympus Stylus for automatic ease or a Canon AE-1 for manual learning. Load Kodak Gold 200 for your first real roll and find a mail-in lab.

Why Film, in 2026?

Walk into any thrift store in America and the film cameras that sat untouched for a decade are gone. Check eBay prices for a Canon AE-1 from five years ago — then check them today. Something is clearly happening.

Most of the people driving this resurgence are in their 20s, shooting film for the first time. They're not nostalgic for something they remember. They're reaching for something that digital doesn't give them: friction. Slowness. The knowledge that this frame cost something, so you'd better mean it.

The imperfections of film — the grain, the slightly soft focus of old lenses, the color shifts — aren't bugs. They're textures that make a photograph feel like it was made in the physical world rather than computed.

"Digital solved the wrong problem. It made photography perfect. And in doing so, removed something important."

Olympus Stylus — Best Point-and-Shoot

The Olympus Stylus (also known as the MJU II in Europe) is the point-and-shoot with a cult following, and it deserves every bit of it. The 35mm f/2.8 lens is sharp. The autofocus is fast. It fits in a jacket pocket and weighs almost nothing.

You load a roll, press the shutter, and it handles everything else. No settings to think about. The photos look beautiful in a way that seems disproportionate to how easy the camera is to use.

The downside: prices have risen sharply. Expect to pay $200-400 for a clean one. If that feels like too much, the Canon Sure Shot series offers similar capabilities for considerably less.

Canon AE-1 — Best for Learning Manual

The Canon AE-1 is the most recognizable film camera in the world — millions were made between 1976 and 1984, and they're still everywhere. It's an SLR, which means you look through the actual lens when you compose, and you control aperture and shutter speed manually (with an automatic mode when you want training wheels).

Learning on a manual camera teaches you to see light differently. When you have to set your own aperture, you start to notice whether a scene is bright or dim, flat or contrasty. That's a skill that transfers everywhere.

Budget $80-150 for a clean one. Make sure the light seals are intact (foam deteriorates over decades — sellers will usually mention if they've been replaced).

Disposable Cameras — The Easiest Start

Kodak and Fujifilm still make disposable cameras. They cost around $15. You point and shoot and hand the whole thing to a lab when you're done. The photos have a lo-fi charm that's genuinely appealing — not because of any technical excellence, but because of what they capture: the grab-shot energy of someone who isn't overthinking it.

Film Stocks to Know

Kodak Gold 200 — Warm tones, affordable, available at CVS. Start here. Kodak Ultramax 400 — Faster, handles lower light, slightly more grain. Kodak Portra 400 — The professional portrait film. Beautiful skin tones. More expensive. Ilford HP5 — The standard black and white film. Extremely forgiving. Push it to 1600 ISO in low light.

Getting It Developed

Mail-in labs are the easiest option for most people. The Darkroom and Indie Film Lab both do excellent work. Mail your roll, they develop and scan it, send you digital files. Roughly $15-25 per roll including scanning. Turnaround is 1-2 weeks.

If you have a local lab, use them. Supporting local labs keeps the infrastructure alive for everyone.
