Why Everyone Is Buying Film Cameras Again

The bottom line

Film is back because digital solved the wrong problem. It made photography perfect, and in doing so removed something important. The people buying film cameras in 2026 aren't nostalgic — they're reaching for friction, slowness, and photographs that feel made rather than computed.

Walk into any thrift store and the film cameras that sat untouched for a decade are gone. Check eBay prices for a Canon AE-1 from five years ago — then check them today. Something is clearly happening.

The Numbers

Film camera sales have increased every year since 2015. Kodak restarted production of Ektachrome. Fujifilm keeps discontinuing film stocks and then un-discontinuing them due to demand. New film cameras — actual new ones, not vintage — are being manufactured again for the first time in decades. Most buyers are in their 20s, shooting film for the first time.

"This isn't nostalgia. The people buying film cameras in 2026 are reaching for something digital doesn't give them."

The Imperfection Problem

Digital photography solved the wrong problem. It made photography perfect — perfectly sharp, perfectly exposed, instantly reviewable, instantly deletable. And in solving that problem, it removed something important. The slight softness of older lenses, the grain in shadow areas, the color shifts when you push film beyond its rated speed — these aren't bugs. They're textures of a photograph made in the physical world.

Slowing Down

Film forces patience. You have 36 exposures. Each one costs about a dollar. You wait days to see results. Those constraints impose mindfulness that's genuinely valuable and impossible to fake with digital. People who shoot film describe looking at the world differently — noticing light, waiting for the right moment, caring about composition in a way that the delete-and-reshoot safety net makes harder to develop.

The Community

The film photography community on Reddit (r/analog, r/filmphotography) is enormous, helpful, and genuinely enthusiastic. One of the least gatekeepy corners of the photography world. Beginners are welcomed, questions are answered. People share both their successes and their overexposed disasters with equal enthusiasm.

Is It Expensive?

Yes, more than it was. A roll of Kodak Portra 400 plus development and scanning runs $25-35. Most film shooters compensate by shooting less and more deliberately — which circles back to the whole point. Start with Kodak Gold 200 or Ultramax 400. Both are affordable, widely available, and produce beautiful results in good light.
