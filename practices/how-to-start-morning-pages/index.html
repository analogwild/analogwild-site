How to Start Morning Pages (and Why It Might Change Everything)

The bottom line

Morning pages are three longhand pages written first thing in the morning — no agenda, no editing, no rereading. The point is not what you write. The point is the act of writing, every day, before the noise starts. Most people who do them for a month notice something shifting. Many do them for years.

What Morning Pages Actually Are

Julia Cameron introduced morning pages in her 1992 book The Artist's Way, and they've quietly been changing people's creative lives ever since. The practice is almost aggressively simple: every morning, before you do anything else, write three pages by hand. Stream of consciousness. No editing. No rereading (at least for the first eight weeks). Don't stop to think. Just write.

That's it. There are no other requirements.

The pages can be about anything: what you dreamed, what's worrying you, what you're having for breakfast, a complaint about your neighbor, a half-formed idea, the color of the light coming through the window. The content is almost beside the point.

"Morning pages are not about writing. They're about clearing. Think of them as draining the swamp of your subconscious."

Why They Work

The theory is that most of us walk around with a constant internal censor — the voice that says your ideas aren't good enough, that you're not a real artist, that you shouldn't try. Morning pages don't silence that voice (nothing does, really) but they do tire it out. Three pages of unfiltered writing burns through the critic's fuel before the day begins.

What you're left with, often, is a quieter mind. Some days a clearer sense of what you actually want. Sometimes an idea you didn't know you had until it appeared on the page.

The research on expressive writing (James Pennebaker's work is the most cited) supports the basic mechanism: writing about your thoughts and feelings, without editing or performing, tends to reduce stress, improve mood, and free up cognitive resources for other things.

How to Start

Step 1: Get a notebook and a pen you like. Not expensive — just ones you don't hate. The ritual matters. A notebook on your nightstand signals that this is the first thing, before the phone, before the coffee, before the news.

Step 2: Set an alarm 30 minutes earlier than usual. Three pages takes most people 20-30 minutes by hand. Give yourself the time. Don't squeeze it.

Step 3: Write before you look at your phone. This is the hardest part and the most important part. The phone primes your brain with other people's agendas. Pages prime it with your own.

Step 4: Don't stop when it's hard. There will be mornings when you write "I have nothing to say I have nothing to say I have nothing to say" for half a page. Write it anyway. Something usually breaks through.

The Only Real Rules

Three pages. Longhand. Every morning. Don't reread for the first eight weeks. That's genuinely all.

Cameron is emphatic about the "no rereading" rule early on because the point is not to create something — it's to express something. Reading back introduces the critic. Let the pages be private, even from yourself, for a while.

Common Questions

What if I miss a day? Start again the next day. This is a practice, not a streak. Missing a day is information (what happened?) not failure.

Do they have to be in the morning? Cameron is firm about this. The morning quality — before you've been shaped by the day — is specific and important. Evening pages are something else (also valuable, different effect).

What do I do with them? Nothing, ideally. Some people shred them. Some fill boxes with them. Some read back occasionally after a few months. The writing is the point, not the artifact.

How long until I notice something? Most people report something shifting within two to four weeks. The changes are often subtle at first — slightly less inner noise, a few better ideas, a little more patience with themselves.
