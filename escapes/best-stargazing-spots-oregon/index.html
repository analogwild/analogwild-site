Best Stargazing Spots in Oregon and What to Bring

The bottom line

Oregon's best dark skies are at Steens Mountain (most remote, most dramatic) and Crater Lake (most accessible, most iconic). For a quick trip from Bend, the Ochocos are 45 minutes away and genuinely dark. Bring red light, warm layers, and something hot to drink.

There's a particular quality to seeing the Milky Way properly — when you're far enough from city lights that the sky has actual texture, that it looks three-dimensional, that you can understand why people built religions around it. Oregon has more of those skies than almost anywhere in the lower 48.

Crater Lake National Park

Sits at 6,000 feet elevation with no major cities for a hundred miles. The Milky Way reflects in the lake on clear nights in a way that photographs can't fully convey. The Rim Drive viewpoints are accessible and the park offers ranger-led stargazing programs in summer. Stay at Crater Lake Lodge to wake up with the lake right there.

Steens Mountain

Southeastern Oregon. One of the least-visited and darkest places in the state. The summit road takes you to nearly 10,000 feet, above any light pollution. The Alvord Desert below is a dry lake bed that extends for miles — lie on it at night and the sky is overwhelming. Camping is primitive and free throughout the area.

"Lie on the Alvord Desert at night and the sky is overwhelming. You can understand why people built religions around this."

Ochoco National Forest

45 minutes from Bend. Excellent dark skies without a major expedition. Ochoco Reservoir and surrounding forest roads have minimal light pollution. The juniper and ponderosa landscape is beautiful in its own right. The best option for a spontaneous night out from Central Oregon.

Painted Hills, John Day Fossil Beds

Already one of Oregon's most visually striking landscapes in daylight — the banded red and gold hills look painted. At night, under dark skies, the experience adds another dimension. The nearby town of Mitchell has a small dark sky park.

Jordan Valley

The high desert of eastern Oregon near Jordan Valley is among the darkest places in the state. Remote, requiring a real drive, basically unpopulated. If you want to feel genuinely far from everything, this is it.

What to Bring

Red headlamp — Red light preserves your night vision, which takes 20-30 minutes to develop and is ruined instantly by white light. Essential.

Shop: Red Headlamp on Amazon

Warm layers — Oregon nights at elevation are cold even in July. More layers than you think. A blanket or sleeping pad — you'll want to lie on your back for extended periods. Something hot in a thermos. Leave your phone face-down once you've identified constellations. The point is to be there, not to document being there.
