Sun Prints and Cyanotype for Beginners: The Most Magical Analog Craft

The bottom line

Buy a sun print paper kit. Go outside. Lay some leaves on it. Wait five minutes in sunlight. Rinse in water. Marvel. This is the fastest path from "analog craft" to "I need to do this again immediately."

If you have never made a sun print, clear your afternoon. This is one of those crafts where the result genuinely surprises you every time — where children and adults have the same reaction when the image appears, where you find yourself immediately wanting to make another one.

What Is Cyanotype?

Cyanotype is a photographic printing process from 1842 — one of the earliest photographic techniques, invented by Sir John Herschel and famously used by botanist Anna Atkins to create the first photographically illustrated book. The chemistry involves compounds sensitive to UV light. Wherever light hits, the paper turns Prussian blue. Wherever something blocks the light, it stays white.

"Children are astonished every time the image appears in the water. So, honestly, are the adults."

What You Need

Sun print paper kits come pre-coated and ready to use. No chemistry mixing required.

Shop: Sun Print Paper Kit on Amazon

Beyond the paper: objects to print (plants work beautifully), a piece of glass or acrylic to flatten everything, a sunny day, a tray of water, and a flat surface to work on in shade.

How to Do It

Work in shade when handling the paper — it's sensitive to UV. Arrange your objects on the paper. Place glass on top to flatten them. Carry the whole thing into direct sunlight. Leave for two to five minutes. The paper will turn lighter as it exposes — this is correct. When it looks almost white, bring it inside and immediately submerge in water. Watch as the image reverses: exposed areas turn deep blue, protected areas emerge white. Let it dry flat. The blue deepens as it dries.

What to Print

Fresh plants are ideal — the translucency of petals creates beautiful gradations. Flat objects with interesting silhouettes work well: keys, scissors, lace, small toys. Try writing on acetate with black marker for text prints. Try layering transparent objects for complexity.

On Fabric

You can do cyanotype on fabric — pre-treated cotton is available — opening up possibilities for tote bags, napkins, tea towels. Same process, but wash in cold water rather than soaking in a tray.

With Kids

Works beautifully from about age 4 up. Let them choose the objects, help arrange them, carry the board outside. The waiting is brief enough to hold attention. The reveal — watching the image appear in the water — is genuinely magical every time, even for adults who know exactly what's going to happen.
